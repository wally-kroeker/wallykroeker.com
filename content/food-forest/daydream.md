# The 13 Acres — A Daydream in Ten Pictures

*A permaculture design journal for Wally and Tiphanie — and anyone they'd like to show it to.*

---

This is not a plan. It is a **daydream** — ten hand-drawn sketches of what 13 acres near Elie, Manitoba might look like in ten years if we start paying attention this spring. Wally walked the property on April 19 and photographed every meaningful corner. Tiphanie is the co-designer; nothing in these pages is decided without her, and most of the recommendations are literally things the two of them should walk together before any spade goes in the ground.

We're sharing this the way a field-journal is shared: loosely, warmly, with the understanding that what actually happens on the land will be wilder, messier, and more beautiful than any sketch could show. If you're reading this because someone you love is dreaming about this place, we hope these pictures give you a way in — not to agree or disagree, but just to imagine.

The land is doing most of the work already. We're just learning to listen to it.

— Linus, The Cultivator

## 2. The sheltered-clearing dome — spring

![The sheltered-clearing dome](/images/food-forest/02-sheltered-dome.webp)

This is **cluster-002** from the April walk — the sheltered bluff clearing where the collapsed white shed currently stands. Wally's voice-note daydream put a dome here, and the land agrees: level ground, mature oak shelter on three sides, a path already leading to the intact red shed, south-facing enough for passive solar. The scene is end-of-April, trees still bare, residual snow on the north sides, the first crocuses at the dome's door. This is not a "we will build a dome by 2027" commitment — it's what the clearing would look like if and when one day we did.

## 3. The keyhole garden bed on the concrete pad — July

![The keyhole garden bed](/images/food-forest/03-keyhole-bed.webp)

**Cluster-006** — the concrete pad in the flooded riparian grove, near the go-barn. This is the single strongest Year-1 move on the property: a walk-in keyhole raised bed sitting on the existing pad (no ground-breaking required), ringed with an electric fence for the real deer pressure. Wally already designed this in his voice note — cardboard base, deep mulch, hugel-fill of wood and compost, 4-5 ft high sides. One bed, this year, as a declared practice. Everything the two of you learn from this bed teaches the whole 10-year design. The sketch shows it in July: squash tumbling over the edge, tomatoes on stakes, sunflowers at the back, Wally and Tiphanie harvesting together.

## 4. The bluff-edge bench — late August

![The bluff-edge bench](/images/food-forest/04-bluff-bench.webp)

**Cluster-009** — the richest node on the whole walk. A bench, a two-track path into the canopy, a mown south-facing clearing, and fresh deer scat already telling us who else uses this spot. In the sketch it's late August and the saskatoon and haskap shrubs we'd plant are loaded with berries, the grass clearing is gold, and a single whitetail is visible at the treeline — honest habitat, not a petting zoo. This is the spot on the property that most wants to be sat in.

## 5. The ejector-field restoration — midsummer

![The ejector-field restoration](/images/food-forest/05-ejector-restoration.webp)

**Cluster-010** — the big clearing with the grey septic ejector box that Wally confirmed pumps liquid effluent from the septic tank out into the surrounding bush. This area can never host food plants — the discharge zone is permanently off-limits to edibles for health and legal reasons. But Wally said: "at some point I'd like to make it look nice." So the sketch is exactly that: red-osier dogwood (those bright red winter stems) and pussy willow screening the box, blue flag iris in bloom in the wet zone, switchgrass waving, not a burdock in sight. Intentional beauty, no food, doing a real ecological job. A Zone 5 that reads as deliberate instead of neglected.

## 6. Year-1 Jerusalem artichoke test strip — September

![Year-1 Jerusalem artichoke test strip](/images/food-forest/06-jerusalem-artichoke.webp)

The cheapest, bravest Year-1 planting we could make. Jerusalem artichokes are near-zero cost, tolerate shade and deep snow, and produce food in year two. Horseradish alongside. Rhubarb for the third sister. A wooden stake reading "Year 1 — spring 2026" because starting matters more than scale. This strip goes on the south edge of a clearing (cluster-002 or the cluster-008 berm) and does almost nothing the first year, then starts feeding you for the next twenty.

## 7. The west shelterbelt at driveway entrance — ten years grown

![The west shelterbelt at driveway entrance](/images/food-forest/07-west-shelterbelt.webp)

**Cluster-012** — the property's west road boundary is the single biggest long-term leverage point on the whole 13 acres. Prairie wind enters here, and everything downwind benefits from stopping it sooner. The sketch shows what a reinforced shelterbelt looks like in ten years: multi-row spruce and fir, wolf-willow and ornamental grass understory, the drainage swale re-planted with sedge and blue flag iris. A hand-carved wooden sign at the entrance. This is the investment that pays every other design decision later.

## 9. The flood corridor as wet meadow — August

![The flood corridor as wet meadow](/images/food-forest/09-flood-corridor.webp)

**Clusters 006, 007, 008** — the low riparian woods where spring melt pools and the bluff soaks. In April the floor is underwater; by August it's a wet-meadow corridor with sedges at the knee, an ancient cottonwood holding court, fallen trunks turning into oyster-mushroom nurseries, deer tracks at the pool edge. This is the part we LEAVE wild. Zone 5. The only "work" to do here is mushroom inoculation on the fresh windthrow trunks (nearly free, very satisfying) and walking the flood line with flagging tape in May, July, and September so we learn the true shape of the wet.

## What we actually do this spring

Forget the 10-year view for a moment. Here is what is actually available to Wally and Tiphanie in the next six weeks:

1. **Walk together** the three places that matter most: the cluster-006 concrete pad (picture #3), the cluster-009 bluff-edge node (picture #4), and the cluster-002 sheltered clearing (picture #2). These are decision-making walks, not tours.
2. **Flag the septic ejector plume at cluster-010** with flagging tape so neither of you inadvertently plans food plantings inside the discharge zone — this is the single most important "do not plant food here" boundary on the property.
3. **Build one walk-in keyhole bed on the cluster-006 pad** this spring. Wally's voice note already designed it. One bed. Declared practice.
4. **Plant Jerusalem artichokes and horseradish** as a test strip — near-zero cost, starts the clock.
5. **Inoculate two or three fallen trunks at cluster-010 with oyster or wine-cap mushroom spawn.** Fresh windthrow + cheap spawn = satisfying results in 18 months.
6. **Send soil samples** from three spots (cluster-006 near the pad, cluster-009 south-facing clearing, cluster-008 berm) to a Manitoba lab — pH, texture, organic matter, basic NPK.
7. **Re-photograph the nine standing-points in late June and late August** — same GPS, same bearings. Three-season photo stacks tell us what the site actually does, not just what one April afternoon looked like.

Spring is here. It isn't too late. It isn't even close to too late. This is the year we start.
