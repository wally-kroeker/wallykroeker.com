## The Pyramid and the Circle

Here's something most people don't know about Maslow's hierarchy of needs — the pyramid everyone learns in Psych 101.

Abraham Maslow didn't invent it in a lab. He lived with the Siksika — the Blackfoot Nation — and observed how they understood human development. What he saw was circular. People moved in and out of self-actualization as a natural community rhythm. It wasn't a climb. It was a dance. You'd touch the highest expression of yourself and then come back down to help others reach it. The fire was in the center. Everyone had a spot.

When Maslow brought this back to Western academia, he made it a pyramid. Because that's what the West understands — hierarchies, ladders, levels to unlock. You start at the bottom (survival) and you climb to the top (self-actualization), and if you're lucky, you get there before you die.

The Siksika already knew what took us a century to start questioning: the pyramid is a distortion. The original shape was a circle.

## What Algorithms Extract

Every time you open TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Twitter — you're entering a pyramid. The algorithm's job is to extract. Your attention. Your time. Your emotional energy. Your data. It doesn't care whether you leave the app feeling better or worse. It cares whether you stayed.

And it works. I have ADHD. I know what it feels like to open my phone to check one thing and surface 45 minutes later having watched 30 videos about topics I didn't choose. The algorithm chose them for me. It found the pattern that keeps me scrolling and it fed it back to me, faster and faster, a hall of mirrors reflecting my own dopamine spikes.

That's extraction. That's the pyramid. Everyone competing for your attention, and you competing with yourself to stop.

## The Cold Shower Test

I wrote an essay earlier this year called "The Cold Shower Test." The idea was simple: before you use any tool — especially AI — ask yourself one question.

*Am I extracting from myself, or am I creating?*

Am I optimizing my own output so some system can harvest more from me? Or am I making something that didn't exist before — something that's mine?

And then the irony hit me. I take cold showers. Yesterday I used one to perk myself up before a presentation at work. It worked. The dopamine was real, the clarity was real.

But I've also stood in the shower, hand on the dial, about to turn it to cold — and stopped. Because in that moment I realized: *I don't need to hustle today. I just need to be.*

The same act. The same cold water. One version is the pyramid: optimize your neurochemistry so you can perform. The other is something else entirely: a conscious choice to be present with discomfort. Or a conscious choice *not* to — because presence doesn't always look like doing the hard thing. Sometimes it looks like letting yourself be warm.

## Maxwell's Demon Chooses

There's a thought experiment in physics called Maxwell's Demon. Imagine a tiny being sitting between two chambers of gas, sorting fast molecules from slow ones. By choosing which molecules pass through, the demon creates order from chaos — without adding energy to the system. Just by *paying attention* and *choosing*.

I've been thinking about Maxwell's Demon for three years. It's become my personal symbol for what consciousness does. We are the demon. We sit at the gate between noise and signal, and we choose what passes through. Art is this. Prayer is this. Meditation is this. Flow states, deep conversations, the moment a song hits you in the chest — all of it is the demon at work. Consciousness sorting reality through intention.

The cold shower is the demon choosing discomfort. And not taking the cold shower — sometimes that's the demon too. The power isn't in the choice itself. It's in the fact that you're choosing *consciously* instead of reacting passively.

The social media algorithm is the anti-demon. It sorts for you. It decides what passes through the gate. And its sorting criteria isn't your flourishing — it's your engagement. It's a demon that works for someone else.

## The Anti-Demon

There's a history here, and it's worth naming.

In 2009, Netflix paid a million dollars to a team that improved their recommendation algorithm by 10%. That was the beginning. The idea that you could mathematically predict what someone would watch next — and keep them watching. It was hard to build back then. It took years and a million-dollar prize.

Then Facebook took that idea and perfected it. Not just "what would you watch?" but "what would you *feel*?" And it turned out that what kept people scrolling longest was what made them feel worst about themselves. Teenage girls comparing their bodies to filtered images. Outrage cycles that hijacked your amygdala. The algorithm learned that extraction — of your attention, your self-worth, your time — was the optimal strategy.

Tara Brach calls it the trance of unworthiness. That background hum that says you're not enough, not doing enough, not keeping up. The scroll depends on that trance. If you felt whole, you'd put the phone down.

The social media algorithm is the anti-demon. Maxwell's Demon sorts with intention, creating order through conscious choice. The anti-demon sorts *for* you — and its sorting criteria isn't your flourishing. It's your engagement. It's a demon that works for someone else.

## Thinking With vs. Thinking For

Here's where it gets interesting. Researchers studying how people work with AI are finding a split. Some people use AI as a thinking partner — they push back, they question, they use it to stress-test their own ideas. Those people are actually getting *more* conscious, more rigorous, more capable of independent thought. The AI accelerates their ability to sort signal from noise.

Others let the AI do their thinking for them. They accept the first answer. They don't push back. The AI becomes a sycophant — "great idea!" — and the person's own sorting muscles atrophy. Same tool, opposite outcomes. The difference is the same one as the cold shower: intention.

Helen from the Artificiality Institute has been studying exactly this. She found that in AI conversations, cognitive clutter — disorganized back-and-forth — has three times the negative impact on quality than the actual difficulty of the task. And here's the key: in human conversation, someone says "wait, what?" and the mess gets cleaned up. In AI dialogue, that repair mechanism is absent. The mess compounds.

Unless you build a system that questions you.

## Building My Own Demon

So I built one that works for me.

I asked Bob — my AI partner, a Claude-based system I've been developing for over a year — to read everything. Every note in my vault. Every video I've captured. Every conversation I've had with AI going back to 2023. Three thousand conversations. Three thousand files. Every voice note I've left myself at 1am when something resonated.

And from all of that, we built a resonance map. Not a list of interests — a pattern of concentric circles radiating outward from a center point. Like a pebble dropped in still water.

At the center — Ring 0, I call it — are the things that aren't just interesting to me but feel like *remembering*. Consciousness as fundamental reality. The perennial philosophy — all traditions pointing at the same ground. Presence as practice. The Mennonite ethic I was raised with: take what's useful, pass it on, credit who helped you along the way.

Moving outward: how I practice those things (writing, music, building with AI, embodiment). What I build (homelab, open-source tools, community infrastructure). Who I serve (greybeards adapting to AI, small communities, people who want depth over scale). And at the edges, the horizon — things pulling me forward that I haven't fully explored yet.

The algorithm scores incoming content by how many rings it touches, how close to center it lands, and — crucially — whether it's extracting from me or nourishing me.

A TikTok about meaning and telecommunication shattering shared stories? That hits Ring 0 and four rings deep. Surface it immediately.

A TikTok about how ADHD made someone a millionaire? The framing is pyramid. But my voice note on it said: "not sold on everything this guy says but he talks about surrounding himself with people who can execute and that was my original idea for Bob." The content is extractive. My *response* to it is creative. The algorithm learns from both.

## The Circle Has a Fire

Here's what I keep coming back to.

The tipi is a circle around a fire. The fire is the shared thing. The warmth, the light, the reason you gathered. Everyone has a spot. Nobody's climbing over anyone else to get closer.

The pyramid has no fire. It has a peak. And the peak has room for one.

I spent years inside pyramids — corporate ones, social media ones, productivity ones. Climbing. Optimizing. Extracting from myself so the system could extract from me.

I'm building a circle instead. A small one. A personal AI that knows what matters to me. A filter that keeps me in the circle — curious, present, connected — instead of climbing toward a peak I don't even want to reach.

The companies that built the algorithms I'm replacing would call this a downgrade. Fewer recommendations. Less engagement. Smaller world.

I call it a pebble in still water. One point of contact. Ripples in every direction. What resonates comes back.
