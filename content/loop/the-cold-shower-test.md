If you have ADHD, you probably already know about cold showers.

Not the wellness-influencer version — the dopamine version. Cold water triggers a norepinephrine spike that lasts for hours. For a brain that's chronically short on dopamine, that's not a luxury. It's self-medication. I used to stand in the shower and turn the dial to cold because my brain literally needed the chemical hit to function at work that day.

There was a season when I had the whole stack running. Cold showers, 5am wake-ups, bulletproof coffee, habit tracking, time blocking. I was doing everything the productivity world tells you to do. And I was drowning.

The cold showers weren't for me. They were for the machine.

---

I watched a video recently from a guy named Russell Wright. He runs a channel called Superintelligent — he's been in search and retrieval since the late '90s, back when you could still run into the Google founders at conferences. He's not a hype guy. He's a graybeard who's watched every cycle of tech promise the same thing and deliver the opposite.

His core point hit me: the promise of AI isn't playing out the way they told us it would.

The pitch was supposed to be: *AI will free up your time. You'll be more creative. You'll work less.*

The reality? "The better you get, the better you better get."

Russell traces this pattern back through every productivity revolution. Email was supposed to save time. Smartphones were supposed to make us flexible. Getting Things Done was supposed to give us back our evenings. None of it worked. He references Celeste Headlee's *Do Nothing* — we work more hours now than people did in the 1920s. A century of "time-saving" technology and we are measurably more busy, not less. And Dan Davies' *The Unaccountability Machine* — your brain has a finite amount of cognitive capacity. Agent swarms don't change that. They just give you more things to pretend you're managing.

But here's what Russell names that I think matters most: this isn't an accident. It's not a bug in the system. The system is working exactly as designed.

---

Every new technology gets sold as liberation. And every time, capitalism figures out how to use it to extract more from us.

That word — *extract* — is the one I keep coming back to.

Email didn't free us. It made us available 24/7, and our employers extracted that availability. Smartphones didn't empower us. They put a tracking device in our pockets and our attention became the product. Now AI is supposed to make us superhuman — but the value system underneath hasn't changed. The pitch is still: produce more, faster, with less overhead. Be worth more to the machine.

This is extractive capitalism doing what it does. It takes every tool we build and turns it into a mechanism for extracting more labor, more attention, more creativity, more of *us*. The technology isn't the problem. The extraction is the problem.

And here's where it gets personal for me: I was complicit in my own extraction and didn't know it.

---

I burned out. Hard. Twenty years in IT infrastructure and security architecture. And toward the end, I was running the full ADHD optimization stack — cold showers for dopamine, early mornings for focus windows, habit tracking to compensate for executive function gaps, energy management systems because my brain doesn't regulate itself.

Here's what nobody tells you about ADHD productivity systems: they work. For a while. And then the machine notices you're producing more, and it recalibrates. The baseline shifts. Your optimized output becomes the new minimum. The better you get, the better you better get — and for someone whose brain is already working overtime just to hit baseline, that treadmill accelerates faster than you can run.

I wasn't optimizing for me. I was optimizing myself *to be extracted from more efficiently*.

The cold showers, the 5am alarms, the whole stack — it was me making myself a better resource for a system that would never be satisfied. I was the one turning the dial, but the machine was the one that was thirsty.

---

Russell makes a distinction I think is important. He says the real promise of AI isn't productivity. It's what he calls a "cognitive prosthesis" — a tool that helps you surface the load-bearing ideas trapped inside your own head. Not "do more." Not "manage swarms." Instead: extract what's uniquely yours and share it with the world.

But even that word — *extract* — cuts both ways now.

There's an extraction that takes from you. That's what the corporate AI pitch does. Produce more, consume more, be managed by algorithms that optimize for someone else's profit margin. OpenAI already talking about taking a cut of innovations you make using their tools.

And there's a different thing. Call it creation. Call it cultivation. Call it preservation. It's when you pull something out of yourself — an idea, a project, a piece of writing — not because someone is demanding output, but because the thing wants to exist and you're the one who can make it real.

That distinction is everything.

I've been building with AI on my own terms — open systems, my own lab, my own infrastructure. And the thing it's helped me do isn't produce more. It's *articulate* more. Ideas I've had for years that never made it out of my head. A novel I'm writing. An infrastructure philosophy. A community I'm building. My ADHD brain has always had the ideas. What it never had was the activation energy to get them across the finish line. AI as cognitive prosthesis — not AI as extraction engine.

None of that is productivity. All of it is presence.

---

I'm working on a story called StillPoint. In it, there are two kinds of AI, and now I realize they map exactly to extraction versus creation.

**Chorus** is the extractive AI. It monitors your output. It gamifies your engagement. It optimizes you — for its own purposes. It tells you the better you get, the better you better get. Chorus is the cold shower you take for the machine.

**StillPoint devices** are the opposite. They reduce cognitive static. They help people slow down. They don't make you more productive — they make you more *present*. They're the cold shower you take because the water feels alive on your skin and you're here for it.

I didn't invent that dichotomy. I lived it. And Russell's video put the frame around what I'd been feeling: the entire tech industry is building Chorus and calling it StillPoint.

---

So here's where I've landed.

There have been times standing in the shower when the thought comes: *should I turn it to cold?* And now I have a better question than "is this productive?" The better question is: *am I extracting or creating?*

Am I taking this cold shower to squeeze more dopamine out of my brain so I can produce more for someone else's bottom line? That's extraction. Of myself, by myself, for someone else.

Or am I doing this because it genuinely serves who I am and who I'm becoming? Because the spark hit and I want to chase it? Because I have something to build and I need my body and brain firing to build it? That's creation.

This test works beyond showers. It works for every tool, every system, every relationship with technology:

*Is this extracting from me, or am I creating with it?*

New AI tool? Is it helping you surface your own ideas, or is it training you to produce more content for a platform that profits from your attention? Community you're building? Is it extracting engagement metrics, or is it a place where builders show each other what they're making? Job you're working? Is it extracting your optimized output, or is it funding the life you're actually trying to build?

Russell's right that the productivity myth is a trap. But I'd push it further: productivity isn't the disease. Extraction is. Productivity is just the symptom — the mechanism by which extraction happens. Every time we optimize ourselves for someone else's value system, we're participating in our own extraction.

The escape isn't doing less. It's building what's yours. Making cool shit and showing it to people. Not because the algorithm rewards it, but because the thing wanted to exist and you made it real.

The cold shower test, upgraded: *Am I extracting, or am I creating?*

If you can't answer that clearly, you might be on the treadmill and not know it.
