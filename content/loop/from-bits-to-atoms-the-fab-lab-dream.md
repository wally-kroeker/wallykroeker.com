# Micro-Manufacturing the Future: A Solarpunk Answer to Trade Wars

*A radically local alternative to tariffs, tech monopolies, and throwaway culture*

As the United States ramps up tariffs—slapping 100%+ duties on electric vehicles and critical tech from China while expanding protectionist policies across global supply chains—it's clear the old model of globalization is cracking. These top-down moves, meant to revive domestic manufacturing, only reinforce the dependence on centralized production, widen inequality, and leave communities more vulnerable to disruption. But what if the answer isn't bigger factories or higher walls? What if the answer is *smaller*—more human-scaled, more resilient, more local?

There's a scene in *A Prayer for the Crown-Shy*, the second solarpunk book about a wandering robot and a tea-serving monk, where a small-town manufacturer quietly fabricates a part the robot needs to keep moving. It's not grandiose. It's not global. It's just right.

That's the vision I've been carrying with me: a **local fabrication lab**—a place where bits become atoms, where we build what we need, when we need it, out of what we have.

### Why Micro-Manufacturing Matters

In a world obsessed with massive factories, global logistics, and throwaway goods, it's strange that micro-manufacturing isn't part of the public conversation. But it should be. Because:

- Tariffs and trade wars won't fix our over-dependence on centralized production.
- Endless consumerism won't restore our relationship with objects—or each other.
- Supply chains break. Local creativity doesn't.

Imagine a future where your town doesn't wait for parts from overseas, but prints or mills them right down the road. Not as survivalism, but as *thrivalism*.

### What the Fab Lab Could Be

This is my dream for a Fab Lab on my land:

- A small but powerful workshop: 3D printers, CNC machines, laser cutters, soldering stations
- Powered by local energy (solar, off-grid backup)
- Run by open-source patterns and AI-assisted co-design
- Stocked with ESP32s, recycled materials, and the occasional weird broken thing that just *might* be useful
- Open to the community—for repairs, for learning, for sharing

It's not about shiny gadgets. It's about **resilience**, **relationship**, and **reclaiming agency** in how we make and maintain the world around us.

### The Balance Wizard Archetype

I've always resonated with the post-apocalyptic character archetype: the one who builds, fixes, and powers the community from junk and ingenuity. The geeky guy who keeps things running when the supply chains are gone. Maybe brings power to the community. Builds what people need from what's lying around.

But my hope is that it doesn't have to be post-apocalypse. Bring the good of the post-consumerist, local, resilient systems to the world *now*—for the betterment of people's lives. Not as a reaction to collapse, but as a gift to the present.

### The Fab Lab Origin Story

The term "Fab Lab" comes from the work of MIT professor Neil Gershenfeld, who founded the Center for Bits and Atoms. His vision was simple but radical: enable ordinary people to build almost anything using digital fabrication tools—turning bits (code, patterns, blueprints) into atoms (real-world objects). This movement, rooted in personal empowerment and technological literacy, gave rise to a global network of Fab Labs: small-scale workshops with 3D printers, CNC machines, and open-source software that can manufacture useful parts, tools, and devices tailored to local needs.

His work inspired my vision and gave it a framework. A Fab Lab isn't just a place to make things—it's a place to rethink production, ownership, and participation in our material world.
